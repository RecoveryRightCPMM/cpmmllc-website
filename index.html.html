Why Your 90837 Keeps Getting Denied — And What to Do About It

— Coastal Practice Medical Management · Behavioral Health Billing Insights

The 90837 is the most commonly billed psychotherapy code in outpatient behavioral health — and it is also one of the most frequently denied. If your 90837 claims are coming back denied, the problem is rarely the service itself.

The vast majority of 90837 denials are fully appealable — and many are recoverable within 30 to 60 days with the right approach.

Most Common Reasons 90837 Gets Denied

  • Missing or expired authorization — Many commercial payers require prior authorization for psychotherapy sessions beyond a certain number.
  • Documentation does not support medical necessity — Your clinical notes must reflect the diagnosis, the patient's functional impairment, and therapeutic goals.
  • Incorrect modifier usage — Billing 90837 without the correct modifier is a common billing error. If a psychiatrist provides medication management and therapy in the same visit, use add-on code 90838.
  • Timely filing exceeded — Most payers have a 90 to 365 day timely filing window from the date of service.
  • Payer requires a different code — Some payers do not reimburse 90837 at all. Always verify payer-specific CPT requirements before billing.

What to Do When Your 90837 Is Denied

  • Pull the Explanation of Benefits and identify the denial reason code (CO, PR, OA, or PI prefix)
  • Cross-reference the denial code with the payer's specific billing guidelines
  • If medical necessity is cited, gather your clinical documentation and build a written appeal
  • If authorization is the issue, contact the payer's provider line to determine whether a retroactive authorization is possible
  • File your appeal within the payer's stated appeal window — typically 30 to 180 days from the denial date

Parity Law

MHPAEA in 2026: How to Use Parity Law to Win Appeals You Think You Have Lost

— Coastal Practice Medical Management · Behavioral Health Billing Insights

The Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act requires insurers to cover behavioral health services on equal terms with medical and surgical care. When a payer denies a mental health claim that would have been approved for a comparable medical service, that denial may be a parity violation.

Parity violations are among the most powerful — and most underused — grounds for overturning behavioral health denials at the internal appeal level.

What MHPAEA Requires

  • Insurers cannot impose treatment limitations on mental health or SUD benefits that are more restrictive than those applied to medical/surgical benefits
  • This covers quantitative limits (number of sessions) and non-quantitative limits (prior auth requirements, medical necessity criteria)
  • Payers must provide a comparative analysis upon request showing how they apply treatment limitations equally

How to Build a Parity-Based Appeal

  • Request the payer's NQTL comparative analysis in writing — they are required to provide it
  • Identify a comparable medical/surgical service that would not face the same limitation
  • Cite the specific MHPAEA provision being violated in your appeal letter
  • Reference the 2024 Final Rule which significantly strengthened enforcement requirements
  • File at Level 1 and escalate to Level 2 if denied — document everything in writing

Modifier 95 vs. Modifier 93: Getting Telehealth Billing Right in 2026

— Coastal Practice Medical Management · Behavioral Health Billing Insights

In 2026, audio-only behavioral health telehealth is permanently reimbursable — but only when billed correctly. Using the wrong modifier, the wrong Place of Service code, or forgetting to document why video was not used will result in an immediate denial.

Modifier 95 and Modifier 93 are not interchangeable. Using the wrong one is one of the most common and most preventable telehealth billing errors in behavioral health.

Modifier 95 vs. Modifier 93

  • Modifier 95 — Use for live video sessions (synchronous audio and video)
  • Modifier 93 — Use for audio-only sessions (telephone or real-time audio only)
  • Place of Service 02 — Use when the patient is at a telehealth originating site other than their home
  • Place of Service 10 — Use when the patient is at home during the telehealth session

Documentation Requirements for Audio-Only

  • Document the specific reason video was not used (patient preference, technology barrier, etc.)
  • Confirm the patient's location at the time of service
  • Verify payer-specific audio-only coverage policies — not all commercial payers cover Modifier 93
  • Check state-specific telehealth parity laws that may require coverage

PA HealthChoices BH-MCO Billing: What Every Provider Needs to Know

— Coastal Practice Medical Management · Behavioral Health Billing Insights

Pennsylvania's behavioral health Medicaid program operates through county-based managed care organizations — and billing rules vary by MCO, by county, and by service type.

PA HealthChoices BH-MCO billing is not one-size-fits-all. What works in Allegheny County may not work in Philadelphia.

The Four Primary BH-MCOs in Pennsylvania

  • Community Care Behavioral Health (CCBH) — Serves Allegheny County and surrounding regions
  • PerformCare — Serves multiple counties in central and western PA
  • Magellan Behavioral Health — Serves Philadelphia and surrounding counties
  • Value Behavioral Health (Value BH) — Serves southwestern Pennsylvania counties

Most Common Billing Issues by MCO

  • Prior authorization requirements vary significantly — always verify before the first session
  • Service definitions and covered CPT codes differ by MCO and county
  • Documentation standards are MCO-specific and are audited regularly
  • Credentialing delays cause retroactive denials — track your effective dates carefully

Solo Therapist's Guide to Insurance Billing: What to Do When Payers Push Back

— Coastal Practice Medical Management · Behavioral Health Billing Insights

Solo practitioners are often left to navigate insurance billing entirely on their own. The most common denial types for independent therapists are entirely predictable, and most have clear, straightforward responses.

Knowing what to do in the first 30 days after a denial makes the difference between recovered revenue and a permanent write-off.

Five Most Common Denials for Solo Therapists

  • Credentialing not effective — The claim was submitted before your effective credentialing date. Always confirm your effective date in writing before billing.
  • Medical necessity not established — Your session notes do not clearly document functional impairment, treatment goals, or clinical rationale.
  • Duplicate claim — A previous claim for the same date of service is already on file.
  • Timely filing exceeded — The claim was submitted outside the payer's filing window.
  • Service not covered — The patient's plan does not cover outpatient psychotherapy or requires a referral.

Your First 30 Days After a Denial

  • Read the denial reason code on the EOB carefully — do not guess
  • Call the provider line to confirm the specific reason and ask about the appeal window
  • Gather your documentation: session notes, treatment plan, prior auth if applicable
  • Submit a written Level 1 appeal within the payer's stated window
  • Track every communication in writing — dates, rep names, reference numbers

Most behavioral health practices have no idea how much revenue is sitting in their denial queue — unworked, aging, and approaching timely filing deadlines.

Industry benchmarks suggest that behavioral health practices lose between 5% and 15% of collectible revenue annually to unworked denials.

Your 5-Minute Billing Audit

  • Top 3 denial reason codes — These three codes typically account for 70-80% of your denial volume.
  • Timely filing deadlines — Check your oldest open claims. Are any approaching the payer's timely filing window?
  • Write-off rate — How much are you writing off monthly? Compare to your denial volume — there may be recoverable revenue in your write-offs.

The February 2026 HIPAA Deadline Your Practice May Have Already Missed

— Coastal Practice Medical Management · Behavioral Health Billing Insights

February 16, 2026 was the federal compliance deadline requiring covered entities to update their Notice of Privacy Practices to reflect major 42 CFR Part 2 changes.

The 42 CFR Part 2 changes effective February 2026 represent the most significant update to SUD confidentiality rules in decades.

What Changed Under 42 CFR Part 2

  • Patient consent for disclosure of SUD records can now be made once for all future treatment, payment, and healthcare operations
  • Covered entities must update their Notice of Privacy Practices to reflect these changes
  • Prohibited uses and re-disclosure rules still apply — the changes expand permitted uses, not eliminate protections

What Your Practice Should Do Now

  • Review and update your Notice of Privacy Practices to reflect 42 CFR Part 2 alignment
  • Update your patient consent forms to reflect the new single-consent framework
  • Train staff on the updated disclosure rules
  • Document your compliance review with a date-stamped record

HIPAA 2026

The HIPAA Security Rule Is Being Rewritten: What Behavioral Health Practices Must Do Now

— Coastal Practice Medical Management · Behavioral Health Billing Insights

The most significant overhaul of the HIPAA Security Rule since its original adoption is underway. For behavioral health practices — where sensitive psychiatric and SUD records are routine — the compliance stakes are especially high.

The proposed updates represent a fundamental shift from flexible "addressable" standards to mandatory, specific technical requirements.

Key Proposed Changes

  • Mandatory MFA — Multi-factor authentication required for all access to ePHI
  • Encryption required — All ePHI must be encrypted at rest and in transit
  • Vulnerability scanning — Required every six months minimum
  • Annual penetration testing — Formal pen testing required annually
  • 72-hour breach notification — Notification to HHS required within 72 hours of discovery

Steps to Take Before the Final Rule

  • Conduct a full risk analysis of all systems that touch ePHI
  • Enable MFA on all ePHI-accessible systems immediately
  • Verify encryption status on all devices, email, and cloud storage
  • Review your Business Associate Agreements — vendors will need to meet the new standards too
